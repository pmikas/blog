Agile Software Development

               These notes are highly based on the course Agile Software Development from edX, taught by Professor Bertrand Meyer.

Content:
  • Agile Manifesto
  • Agile Values
  • Agile Principles


Agile Manifesto

        INDIVIDUALS AND INTERACTIONS over processes and tools
        WORKING SOFTWARE over comprehensive documentation
        CUSTOMER COLABORATION over contract negociation
        RESPONDING TO CHANGE over following a plan

Main Agile methods:
  • XP (Extreme Programming)
  • Lean
  • Crystal
  • Scrum

        Practice: concrete and practical
        Principle: abstract, general, prescriptive
        Assertion: statement


Agile Values

  1. New, reduced role for managers: managers do not assign tasks to project members - sef organized team
  2. No "big upfront" steps: start producing work right away
  3. Iterative development: development is step by sep, very progressive with short iterations
  4. Limited, negotiated scope: limit waste
  5. Focus on quality, achieved through testing


Agile Principles

Organizational:
  1. Put the customers at the center
  2. Accept change
  3. Let the team self-organize
  4. Maintain a sustainable pace
  5. Produce minimal software:
    • Produce minimal functionality;
    • Produce only the product requested;
    • Develop only code and tests.
Technical:
  1. Develop interatively:
    • Produce frequent working iterations;
    • Freeze requirements during iterations.
  2. Treat tests as a key resource:
    • Do not start any new development until all tests pass;
    • Test first.
  3. Express requirements through scenarios

Problems with Waterfall model:
  • Late appearance of actual code;
  • Lack of support for requirements change - and more generally for extendibility and reusability;
  • Lack of support for maintenance activity;
  • Division of labor hampering Total Quality Management;
  • Impedance mismatches;
  • Highly synchronous model.

Seven wastes of software development (Lean view):
  • Extra/unused features (overproduction);
  • Partially developed work not released (inventory);
  • Intemediate/unused artifacts (extra processing);
  • Seeking information (motion);
  • Escaped defects not caught by tests/reviews (defects);
  • Waiting (including customer waiting);
  • Handoffs (transportation).

Closed-Window Rule: during an iteration, no one can add functionality to the list of functionalities agreed upon in advance for that iteration.

Standard form for user stories:
"As a ❬user_or_role❭ I want ❬bussiness_functionality❭ so that ❬business_justification❭"

Major application: validating requirements.

In Scrum there is no "project manager" or "team leader".

Self-organizing team:
  • Cross-functional;
  • Has 7±2 members;
  • Selects iteration goal and work results;
  • Organizes itself and its work;
  • Can do everything within guidelines to reach goal;
  • Demos work results to product owner.

Product Owner:
  • Defines product features;
  • Decides on release dates;
  • Decides on release content;
  • Responsible for product profitability (ROI);
  • Prioritizes features according to market value;
  • Can change features and priority over 30 days;
  • Accepts or rejects work results.

Scrum Master:
  • Ensures that the team is functional productive;
  • Enables cooperation accross all roles & functions;
  • Shields team from external interferences;
  • Enforces process: daily meeting, planning & review meeting;
  • Remove impediments*;
  • Normally, does not develop.
* Impediment: anything that affects the velocity (progress) of the team.
